The Core Problem:

You're winning on volume but losing on visibility. Competitors spend less but appear in premium positions more often. They're not outspending you—they're outplaying you in the auction mechanics.

Why Traditional Competitive Bidding Fails

  • ❌ Direct Brand Bidding Creates Price Wars Bidding on competitor names triggers retaliatory bidding, inflating CPCs for everyone while damaging Quality Scores through low relevance
  • ❌ Blanket Bid Increases Waste Budget Raising all bids uniformly to compete burns budget on low-value positions without strategic impact
  • ❌ Fighting on Their Terms Premium competitors (Bright Horizons) have higher margins and can sustain CPC wars. You need asymmetric strategies
The Winning Strategy: Auction Intelligence Over Brute Force

Dominate auctions through Quality Score optimization, strategic bid layering, and exploiting competitor weaknesses—not through direct confrontation. Win by being smarter, not louder.

Advanced Auction Tactics: Asymmetric Warfare

1. Dayparting Strategy: Avoid Peak Competition

  • 📊 Analysis Required Run hour-of-day report for last 60 days. Identify when auction competition is highest (likely 9am-12pm, 6pm-9pm when parents search)
  • ⚡ Tactical Response Peak Hours (9am-12pm, 6-9pm): Tier 1 keywords only, bid +40% to guarantee position
    Off-Peak Hours (12pm-6pm, 9pm-11pm): Expand to Tier 2 & 3, normal bids (lower competition = cheaper clicks)
    Weekend/Late Night: Test budget allocation when competitors sleep
  • 💰 Expected Impact Capture same volume at 20-30% lower average CPC by timing your aggression. Competitors can't be everywhere at once.

⚠️ Critical Context: £2,584 spent on "0-conversion" search terms in last 30 days, including £1,172 on "nursery near me." Before cutting these as waste, we must understand if they're assisting conversions in multi-touch journeys.

Typical Childcare Search Journey (Hypothesis)

Stage 1: Awareness
"nursery near me" | "childcare options" | "best nurseries [city]"
Search → Click → Browse → Leave
Stage 2: Consideration
"busy bees [location] reviews" | "nursery [postcode] cost" | "Ofsted ratings"

Key Question: Is "nursery near me" wasteful spend, or is it the first touchpoint in a journey that eventually converts via brand search? Attribution analysis reveals the truth.

Step 2: Search Terms Path Analysis

Location: Google Ads → Campaigns → Insights & Reports → Search Terms → Path Analysis
Focus: Top Conversion Paths report

Questions to Answer:
  • How many touchpoints typically occur before conversion? (Avg path length)
  • Does "nursery near me" frequently appear as first touchpoint followed by brand search conversion?
  • What % of converters interact with multiple keywords before converting?
  • Time lag: Days from first click to conversion (expect 3-14 days for childcare decision)

Step 3: Assisted Conversions Report

Location: Google Ads → Tools → Attribution → Assisted Conversions
Filter: Keywords with 0 last-click conversions but >0 assisted conversions

Key Metric: Assisted/Last Click Ratio
  • Ratio > 1: Keyword assists more conversions than it closes (top-of-funnel)
  • Ratio = 1: Balanced role in journey
  • Ratio < 1: More likely to be final conversion touchpoint (bottom-of-funnel)

Step 4: GA4 Cross-Platform Analysis (If Available)

Location: GA4 → Advertising → Attribution → Conversion Paths
Purpose: See if users research via organic search then convert via paid

Cross-Channel Patterns:
  • Do users find you via "nursery near me" (paid), leave, then return via organic brand search?
  • Are there multi-day journeys where paid ads introduce brand, organic closes?
  • Mobile vs. desktop research-to-conversion patterns

⚠️ Common Attribution Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Cutting Top-of-Funnel Too Early: "0 conversions" doesn't mean "0 value" if assisted conversions exist
  • Over-Crediting Brand: Brand searches often convert, but they're the RESULT of earlier touchpoints, not the cause
  • Ignoring Time Lag: If analyzing only 7-day data, you're missing conversions that took 10-14 days to mature
  • Single Attribution Model: Always compare 3-4 models to understand full picture. No single model is "correct"
